GENERAL FAQ

Q.Is the curriculum pre-planned and step by step?

A. Our coding bootcamp focuses on the DOING part of learning. There is no step A, step B, step C, prescribed ahead of time in the real world, and since at our core, we are an Agile organization, we have no time for things pulled from our own rear ends. We like organic solutions, not contrived examples.

What better way to learn to build web apps to do it by building real, interesting web applications, not a Pinterest clone no one will ever use or love. At Devschool you will select (with the help of your Instructor) a topic that interests you, and then you will build a web app related to that.

Q.What kind of students do you accept? Newbies? Mid level?

A. Since opening our doors officially, we have taught people from all walks of life and of all ages. Some students have never written a line of real code in their life, and some students come to us from other Bootcamps or from Universities.

Since our Instructors have a minimum of 10 years in software development and a wide range of abilities and skills, it makes it very easy for us to teach students from many different backgrounds and from various skill levels.

Q.What hours are class held? Is it flexible?

A. Our school is in US Central time and sessions happen mostly between 11am and 4pm, however it is possible to have your one on one's held later in the evening based on Instructor availablitity. We do this for people who work during the day, and for our students east of the Atlantic

Q.How do I work with my Devschool Instructor?

A. You will work directly with your Instructor to plan your web application in the exact same way you would once you are working in the 'real world', figuring out the features needed along the way and how the user interfaces will look.

Each week you will meet with your Instructor via Zoom zoom.us via screen sharing and audio chat. You can broadcast your video if you like, but usually we just do a photo instead of video so your hair can be messy or you can learn in your pajamas, whatever floats your boat.

The number of times you will meet with your instructor weekly is between the two of you, and is scheduled a month in advance. This allows you to adapt your learning experience based on your own ever-changing availability.

Q.What about self study hours? What will I learn on my own?

A. There are a total of 420 hours in the curriculum when you discount the time you spend interning with a non profit. That means 40 of those hours are one on one with your Instructor. The rest is a combination of group sessions, and "self study" hours, in which you hack on your app and the learning takes whatever twists and turns as you run into challenges along the way.

Q.What about learning in a group setting do you have that?

A. Group sessions are available at least once a day, five days a week, and are led by your Instructor. During these pair programming sessions patterns and practices will be demonstrated by your instructor in a wide variety of programming topics.

It is 'participation pair programming' in the sense that if you are following along and you understand the material, shout out answers. Challenge your Instructor's code. Don't quite yet know what is going on? Stay quiet and watch it all unfold.

Q.When do these group pair programming sessions take place?

A. Group sessions happen once a day if there is demand. There must be at least 2 students available for the days group session to take place. Otherwise it wouldn't be much of a group! They happen on a revolving schedule, meaning that each day the group session is at a different time. This allows us to accommodate more diverse student schedules.

Q.Can I record the group and private sessions to re-watch later?

A. Absolutely! In fact we recommend that you create a folder at the very beginning of the course and save a recording of every session you attend there for later viewing. It is like having your own personal coding bootcamp screencast series!

Q.What if I don't know what I want to focus on?

A. Maybe the application you want to build will naturally lend itself to a heavy JavaScript interface, creating a very interactive experience. Maybe it will be more MBA related and have lots of forms in which to enter data, or maybe something more like a marketplace with money changing hands.

One of the greatest strengths of the curriculum at our coding bootcamp is it's ability to adapt based on the interests and needs of each student. Not everyone wants to build the same five web applications to learn.

Q.What text editor will I learn and use and learn to use and love?

A. We start you off learning Vim. It is extremely powerful and more importantly, better suited in our opinion than anything else to write web based software, which is what we are talking about here! Is is great for working on files both locally and on remote machines out there in the interwebs. Sublime can't do that!

Developers that show a proficiency in using expert editors like Vim are proven to make more money and get more job offers. This is because programmers are interviewed by other programmers and managers that used to be developers. They know the difference between someone who 'steals' Sublime vs a developer that put love and attention into their tool-set.

Q.What front-end web developer skills will I learn and focus on?

A. The backbone and foundation of the Internet is HTML and more recently, HTML5. HTML is the structure of the web page, but you also need some style. That is where CSS and again, more recently, CSS3 comes into play. You 'put' an element on a page (like a unordered list) with HTML, you make it look pretty with CSS.

But there is more to it then just the languages. We also need a front-end framework to equalize out all the various differences in browsers and computers. We need something that will allow the pages we build respond to changes, like when we flip our phones or tablets from vertical to horizontal. Or to just deal with the myriad of screen sizes available.

That is where Twitter's Bootstrap front-end framework comes in. We teach you the nitty gritty of responsive design using bootstrap and tools like Masonry, which is how Pinterest gets such awesome looking flowing pages!.

Q.Can I use Windows to learn Web development at your coding bootcamp?

A. Windows is a fine operating system for writing Microsoft software or to use as a business platform, and it makes an excellent gaming platform, likely the best on the market. However it is not great for writing web software that relies on open source libraries and technologies.

We used to advise Windows users to run Linux on a virtual machine, however that turned out to be slow and highly problematic. We are no longer advising people to run virtual machines. It is much better to buy a used Windows laptop and install Linux on it.

Windows does not support the libraries that are needed, and it doesn't share the same architecture. In short Windows speaks a language of it's own, while Linux and OSX both agree to support much of UNIX.

Q.What computer do you recommend for learning web development?

A. Our own statistics show: Mac OSX users have the smoothest web development experience with the tools you will learn here. That being said, we also love Linux if it is run natively and the person wants a real software education and maybe a bit of adventure.

Ubuntu and Mint are popular choices for those who want to strut their kneckbeards, be they man, be they woman. Both Linux and OSX are built on the foundations of UNIX.

Q.Will I learn the 'Server-Side' at your coding bootcamp?

A. Ruby is commonly listed as the second most listed language in all open source job postings. Ruby on Rails is a web framework specifically designed to help you get things done quickly. What kind of things? Web development things!

We also will teach you 'Algorithms' and how to write performant code. You will be able to roll your own APIs, build "web services", and understand the differences in the various types of memory and processors.

Q.Will I learn JavaScript at Devschool?

A. JavaScript is said to be the "Next Big Thing" by many, many smart developers with their fingers on the pulse of the Internet. We also feel that JavaScript is highly important to the future of web development stacks, so we teach a good amount of it once you get some basics under your belt.

Q.What about Payment processing chat apps and things like that?

A. To be an effective developer you need to know a good measure of all of these things to be a candidate worth hiring. And that means a candidate who can wrangle all these tools and languages into technology solutions for businesses.

Along the way you will learn how to leverage Stripe for payments to accept payments, and how to build real time apps like chat, back end management apps, statistics gathering and chart displays, and anything else that comes up when you are building a real app over a span of months or more.

You will also learn about deployment and setup and DNS at Heroku for simple deployment, Amazon Web Services (AWS) for professional-grade services, and CloufFlare for DNS and site speed.

It is a lot to learn, but our job is to guide you through it, so you learn what you need to know, and not get bogged down in the things that might not matter. You can think of the Devschool online developer bootcamp like your technology education flashlight in the dark!

Q.How is it exactly that we meet together online?

A. Devschool utilizes a mixture of technologies to accomplish a "connected" environment and culture for our Students. This is not like Udemy where you feel like you are in it alone. We promote a very social atmosphere.

We use Flowdock for our chat program, but it is really so much more then that! It allows contextual conversations so what you are talking about doesn't get mixed up with other people's messages that have nothing to do with what you are saying.

Q.What about screen-sharing? How does that work exactly?

A. We use a program called Zoom to broadcast our private and group sessions. This allows the Instructor to share his screen and work and demonstrate things with live lessons, or to switch over to a student's machine and fix something for them.

It works best with Mac but there is a version for Linux as well.
